Economic Momentum

Chicago Fed CFNAI
Broad activity, >0 = growth
--
loading

The Chicago Fed National Activity Index (CFNAI) is a "health gauge of the US economy". Think of it as a dashboard light in a car showing how hard the engine is running — not for one company, but for the entire economy. It combines 85 different economic signals into a single number: employment, production, housing, sales, inventories, income, and more.

How do you read it?

0 = normal — The economy is running at its historical average.

Above 0 — Economy running faster than normal. More growth, more activity, companies doing better.

Below 0 — Economy running slower than normal. Less growth, possible slowdown.

Easy comparison:

+0.5 → "The economy feels quite healthy."
0 → "Everything is normal."
-0.5 → "The economy is starting to catch a cold."
-0.7 or lower (sustained) → "Watch out, recession risk rising."

Why investors care about this:

Strong economy (positive) → companies earn more → good for stocks, but central bank keeps rates high.
Weak economy (negative) → rate cuts become likely → sometimes good for tech/crypto, but bad if recession looms.

💡 Tip: don't look at just one month. Investors use the 3-month moving average (CFNAI-MA3) to see the real trend.

In short: this indicator tells whether the US economy is running faster or slower than normal — and gives an early hint of where the economy is heading.

Industrial Production is America's factory and output thermometer. It answers a simple question: how hard is the real economy running? Not the stock market, not AI hype — but: are factories actually making things, are machines running, is energy being consumed?

What does it measure?

How much output is being produced — cars 🚗, machinery ⚙️, chips 💻, steel 🏗️, energy ⚡, mining ⛏️.
Rising → companies are producing more.
Falling → the economy is cooling.

Capacity Utilization — even more interesting:

What percentage of maximum capacity are businesses using? Think of a restaurant:
50% → half-empty, slow.
80% → busy, kitchen running well.
95% → staff stretched, fully packed.

How do you read it?

80%+ → Strong economy, businesses running hard.
75%–80% → Normal / slightly soft.
Below 75% → Warning sign for slowdown or recession.
85%+ → Running too hot — inflation risk.

Why investors care about this:

Strong production + high utilization → economy strong → good for cyclical stocks (industry, energy, banks), but Fed keeps rates high longer.
Falling production → economy cooling → more chance of rate cuts → often favorable for tech/crypto, as long as it doesn't become a severe recession.

Nonfarm Payrolls
MoM change (K jobs)
--
loading

Non Farm Payrolls (NFP) simply asks: "How many new jobs were added in America last month?" — America's job counter.

Why "non-farm"?

Farm jobs don't count — they fluctuate too much seasonally and would distort the picture. Neither do military and self-employed workers. Yet this still measures ~80% of all US jobs.

How do you read it?

+250K → Companies hiring massively. Economy running strong.
+150K → Healthy, normal growth.
+50K → Economy slowing down a bit.
0 or negative → Alarm bell. Recession risk rising.

Why is this so important?

People with jobs spend money. Work → salary → groceries → cars → vacation → companies earn money. More jobs = stronger economy. Fewer jobs = economy cooling.

Investors also watch:

• Unemployment % — rising or falling?
• Wages (Average Hourly Earnings) — higher wages = more spending, but also more inflation.
• Which sectors are growing? Tech? Healthcare? Industry?
• Revisions from prior months — sometimes very different in hindsight.

For stocks and crypto:

Very strong NFP → good for "old economy" (banks, industry), but rates stay high — less good for tech/crypto.
Slightly weaker NFP → often bullish for Nasdaq and crypto — market thinks: rate cut is coming.
Very bad NFP → initial panic ("recession?") → stocks down.

💡 Remember: NFP tells whether America is adding jobs. More jobs = stronger economy. But too strong can actually hurt tech/crypto, because rates stay high.

Retail Sales
YoY % change
--
loading

Retail Sales YoY is America's spending meter: are Americans still spending money in stores — compared to the same month last year?

Simple example:

Last April: $100 spent. This year: $105. Then Retail Sales YoY = +5% → "Americans are buying more than last year."
Think: clothing 👕, cars 🚗, electronics 💻, supermarkets 🛒, restaurants 🍔, online shopping 📦.

This is crucial because consumers are the engine of the US economy — over 70% of the economy runs on what people buy.

How do you read it?

+5% or higher → People spending freely. Economy feels strong.
+2% to +4% → Healthy, normal growth.
Around 0% → People becoming cautious.
Negative → People buying less than last year — economic warning.

The catch:

Too strong retail sales → Fed thinks "economy running too hot → inflation stays high" → rates stay high longer → not good for Nasdaq and crypto.

For stocks and crypto:

Strong, but not too strong → bullish for stocks.
Slightly cooling → often bullish for tech/crypto (rate cut getting closer).
Collapsing → recession fears → bearish.

💡 Remember: when Americans stop shopping, the economy usually starts coughing.

Labor Market — Zeberg "Under Water" Monitor

Unemployment Rate
Lagging, but crucial
--
loading

The Unemployment Rate (UNRATE) is America's unemployment gauge: how many people want to work but don't have a job?

Simple example:

100 people want to work. 96 have a job ✅, 4 are searching ❌ → unemployment = 4%.
Note: retirees, students, and people who stopped searching do not count.

How do you read it?

3%–4% → Very strong economy, jobs easy to find.
4%–5% → Healthy, normal economy.
5%–7% → Economy cooling.
8%+ → Major problems / recession feeling.
10%+ → Crisis level (like 2008 or Covid).

Why the Fed watches this obsessively:

Too low unemployment (e.g. 3%) → wages rise → inflation → rates stay high.
Rising unemployment → economy cooling → rate cut getting closer.

It sounds odd, but rising unemployment isn't always bad:

Slight rise → market thinks "Fed will cut rates" → often bullish for Nasdaq and crypto.
Too much rise → recession fears → bearish for almost everything.

Difference from NFP:

NFP = how many jobs were added (car's speed 🚗)
UNRATE = how many people are jobless (engine temperature 🌡️)
You always look at both together.

💡 Remember: low unemployment = strong economy, but too low can cause inflation and hurt tech/crypto.

IC4WSA is America's layoff meter — but measured smartly. Every week they count how many people file for unemployment benefits for the first time ("help, I lost my job"). That's called Initial Jobless Claims.

Why the 4-week average?

One week can be distorted by snowstorms ❄️, strikes 🚧, holidays 🎄, or temporary shutdowns. The 4-week average filters out that noise so you see the real trend.

Simple example:

Week 1: 210K  |  Week 2: 220K  |  Week 3: 200K  |  Week 4: 230K
→ IC4WSA = 215K

How do you read it?

180K–220K → Few layoffs, strong labor market.
250K–300K → Companies becoming cautious, labor market cooling.
Hard rise above 300K → Alarm 🚨 — early recession warning.

Comparison with other indicators:

NFP = how many people were hired 🚗
UNRATE = how many people don't have a job 🌡️
IC4WSA = are companies starting to lay people off? 🔥

IC4WSA is often the first smoke alarm — it comes out weekly and is much faster than NFP or UNRATE.

For stocks and crypto:

Falling claims → strong economy → bullish for stocks.
Slightly rising → market thinks: rate cut getting closer → bullish for Nasdaq/crypto.
Exploding claims → recession fear → bearish for everything.

💡 Remember: when IC4WSA rises fast, the economy starts coughing before you see it anywhere else.

Sahm Rule Indicator
≥ 0.5 = recession trigger
--
loading

SAHMREALTIME is America's recession smoke alarm — invented by economist Claudia Sahm. The question is simple: is unemployment suddenly rising faster?

The Sahm Rule:

When unemployment rises 0.5% or more above its lowest point of the past year, history says a recession has often already begun.

Example: lowest unemployment of the past year was 3.5% → alarm triggers at 4.0%.

How do you read the value?

Below 0.3 → Nothing alarming.
Heading toward 0.5 → ⚠️ Warning.
0.5 or higher → 🚨 Alarm threshold — historically often already in recession.

Comparison with other indicators:

UNRATE = temperature gauge 🌡️
IC4WSA = first smoke from the engine bay 💨
SAHMREALTIME = dashboard warning light "engine problem" 🚨

Why "REALTIME"?

There are two versions. REALTIME uses only numbers that investors knew at that moment — no after-the-fact revised data. That makes it a fairer signal.

⚠️ Nuance — the 2024 lesson:

In 2024 the alarm briefly triggered without an actual recession. Unemployment rose because more people entered the workforce and due to immigration — not because of mass layoffs. Even Claudia Sahm said: "Take it easy, this time could be different."
Always use this together with IC4WSA and UNRATE — never in isolation.

For stocks and crypto:

SAHM rising toward 0.5 → recession risk grows → Fed may cut rates → bullish for tech/crypto if recession stays mild.
SAHM well above 0.5 + other data deteriorating → recession fears → risk assets can drop hard.

💡 Remember: SAHMREALTIME is a smoke alarm. When unemployment rises too fast, it says: "Uh oh… something may be going wrong."

Liquidity — Why is the market still rising?

Fed Balance Sheet
WALCL — total assets (trillion $)
--
loading

WALCL is America's money tap — the Federal Reserve's total balance sheet. The core question: is the Fed still printing money, or are they pulling money out of the system?

What is the Fed balance sheet?

The Fed is America's superbank. It can buy things: government bonds 🇺🇸, mortgage bonds 🏠, emergency loans to banks 🏦.

When the Fed buys a lot: more money/liquidity enters the system.
When the Fed sells or stops: money leaves the system.

Think of a garden 🌱

The Fed has a water hose.
📈 Balance sheet growing = more water 💧💧💧 = economy and markets get support.
📉 Balance sheet shrinking = less water 🚿 = harder for stocks and crypto.

The two terms you need to know:

QE (Quantitative Easing) — the Fed buys a lot and pumps money into the economy. It did this during the 2008 banking crisis and Covid 2020 → balance went from ~$4 trillion to ~$9 trillion. In plain English: "turning up the money printer."

QT (Quantitative Tightening) — the opposite. The Fed lets bonds mature without buying new ones. Balance sheet shrinks slowly. Since the 2022 peak it has come down significantly.

How do you read it?

Balance rising 📈 → more liquidity → often bullish for Nasdaq, growth stocks, crypto, real estate.
Balance falling 📉 → less liquidity → often headwind for risk assets.

Note: don't panic on a decline. What matters most is how fast the tap is changing.

Currently (~$6.7 trillion):

Well below the Covid peak (~$9 trillion), but still historically enormous. The Fed has been doing QT — slowly draining money from the system. "The tap is no longer wide open like during Covid, but not fully closed either."

The big analogy:

Interest rates = gas pedal/brake 🚗
Fed balance sheet = how much fuel is in the tank ⛽

You can press the gas, but without fuel you won't get far 😄

💡 Remember: WALCL shows whether the Fed is pumping money in or pulling it out. More money in the system is usually good news for stocks and crypto.

M2 Money Supply
YoY % — negative = tight
--
loading

M2SL is America's money-in-circulation meter. The core question: how much money is swimming around in the US economy?

What is M2?

Think of America as one giant swimming pool 🏊 — M2 measures how much "usable money" is in that pool. That includes: cash 💵, money in checking accounts, savings, money market accounts, and small bank deposits.

Why does it matter?

The economy and investments run on liquidity. More money in the system often means: ✅ more borrowing, ✅ more investing, ✅ higher asset prices.
Less money: ❌ less liquidity, ❌ more pressure on stocks/crypto.

How do you read it?

M2 rising fast 📈 → more money in circulation → often bullish for risk assets.
M2 growing slowly → normal economy.
M2 falling 📉 → liquidity being removed → headwind for stocks and crypto.

Covid lesson:

In 2020–2021 M2 shot up fast — the Fed turned on the money printer, people received stimulus checks: "a lot more money entered the pool." In 2022–2023 M2 actually declined for a period, historically rare, due to higher rates and QT.

For stocks & crypto:

Many macro investors watch one simple relationship: M2 up = often good for Bitcoin, Nasdaq, and growth stocks. Extra money seeks returns and flows into tech, AI, crypto.

⚠️ Note the lag: M2 changes first → markets react months later.

Difference from Fed Balance Sheet (WALCL):

Fed balance sheet = water from the tap 🚿 (what the central bank does).
M2 = how much water is ultimately in the pool 🏊 (how much money is actually circulating in the economy).

The tap can be on, but if banks or people borrow less, M2 may still not grow much.

💡 Remember: M2 tells how much money is circulating in the economy. More money = often good for stocks and crypto. Less money = often tougher times for risk assets.

Fed Funds Rate
Effective policy rate (%)
--
loading

FEDFUNDS is arguably the most important economic indicator of all — America's interest rate dial. The core question: how expensive is it to borrow money in America?

The car analogy 🚗

The Fed sits behind the wheel of the US economy. The interest rate is their main control: give gas (lower) or brake (raise).

What is the Federal Funds Rate?

Officially: the rate banks charge each other for short-term loans. But for investors it means: this rate influences almost all other rates — mortgages 🏠, car loans 🚗, credit cards 💳, business loans 🏭, savings rates 💰.

How do you read it?

Low rate (0%–2%) → cheap to borrow → people buy homes, invest, companies hire → often good for Nasdaq, tech and crypto 🚀

Medium rate (3%–5%) → fairly normal economy, not too hot not too cold.

High rate (5%+) → expensive to borrow → people and businesses cautious → headwind for growth stocks and crypto 📉

Why does the Fed change rates?

Inflation too high 🔥 → Fed raises rates → borrowing more expensive → people spend less → economy cools → inflation falls.

Economy weak 🥶 → Fed lowers rates → cheaper to borrow → more spending → economy gets support.

The golden rule for investors:

Lower rates = usually bullish for tech and crypto — when savings pay almost nothing, money flows into stocks, AI, Nasdaq and Bitcoin.
Higher rates = usually bearish for tech and crypto — when savings pay 5%, why take big risks?

Comparison with other indicators:

NFP/UNRATE → "How are jobs doing?"
M2 → "How much money is circulating?"
WALCL → "How much liquidity is the Fed injecting?"
FEDFUNDS → "How expensive is money?"

Many other indicators ultimately point to one question: what will the Fed do with rates?

💡 Remember: FEDFUNDS is America's rate dial. Low rates usually accelerate stocks and crypto. High rates are usually the brake.
